## Bengali to English poetry translation project

**Background**: I started my NLP journey in 2016 working on low-resource languages, particularly Bengali. Since then, 
I've always wanted to work on another project related to it, but never got the chance. So, this summer, I took some 
time to do this passion project using some of the latest tools in NLP!

**Topic** (Initial Attempt): I began this project with an **extremely** ambitious setup - _Translating Rabindranath 
Tagore's poetry to English, but in the style of William Shakespeare._ Naturally, I'd be crazy to attempt something like
this, considering the complexity of the task, i.e., a model has to (i) understand complex Tagore poetry, (ii) map that 
to a different language (English) and, finally, (iii) imitate the style of the _Bard_. To this end, I tried to create
samples where the model is exposed to a similar _themed_ sonnet by Shakespeare, but ultimately, that didn't lead me
anywhere. The lesson learned here is that unless I have the relevant target data, it's pretty much impossible to do 
this task. **I refused to use synthetic data generated by a frontier model because I wanted the data to remain as close
to authentic as possible.**

**Topic** (This Project ✅): I realized that _translating_ Tagore's poetry from Bengali to English is challenging enough. 
As such, I decided to pursue that as a more realistic goal.
___

## Dataset

For this project, I used Rabindranath Tagore's Nobel Prize winning **Gitanjali** collection. He translated 103 poems 
from it into English. However, figuring out the mapping proved more challenging than I thought. This is how I assembled/
scraped the data.

### BN-EN Mapping

This is a crucial step as not knowing which translation is mapped to which Bengali source wouldn't allow us to create 
the dataset. I used the mapping from _https://www.milansagar.com/gitanjali/suchiEng.html_. However, scraping this 
website was a nightmare because it used early anti-scraping tricks. This step created the `gitanjali_mapping.json` file.

### Full Poem Source

- I downloaded the _English_ translations from _Project Gutenberg_ (_https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/7164_). 
Of course, this was much easier to parse as it was cleaner and followed a structured format.
- I obtained the full Bengali poems from _Kaggle_ (_https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/aagalib/complete-works-of-rabindranath-tagore?resource=download_).

### Text Parsing

The titles from the BN-EN mapping allowed me to locate both Bengali/English texts in their respective source. However, 
as the original mapping site (in 1) was so, for the lack of a better word, crap 😆, I had to manually paste 37 Bengali 
poems from _https://www.tagoreweb.in/_. Additionally, the English titles needed manual editing. This is because they 
either had spelling mistakes or used syntax not found in the downloaded version. Finally, after all of this editing, the
entire high-fidelity BN-EN dataset was ready: `parallel_text.csv`.

### Train/Test

For training my model, I wanted to use most of the poems, but leave 1 for evaluation - _Where the mind is without fear_: 
this is the only poem that I can sort of understand deeply 😅.

## Model Training

I tried my best to use my local AMD RX 560 GPU for training. However, I realized that no matter what, it's pretty much
impossible to do any sort of tuning on this card in 2026, as the necessary libraries are not supported by it. So, I 
decided to move my setup to my university cluster - I was only using it when literally nobody else was logged on! As I 
was on the cluster, I had access to a lovely **NVIDIA A100 80GB card**. Thus, I decided to forgo LoRA and did **full
fine-tuning**! This is the first time I did full SFT & I could see that pretty much the entire card was needed for a 
4B Qwen3 model! I used the BASE variant as I wanted to see what behavior I could get without their post-training layer.

I tested 2 models - `Qwen3-0.6B` and `Qwen3-4B`. Both models were trained under the same settings 
(see `model_training.py` for hyperparameters).

## Discussion

I make the following observations,

**(Small quantity + High quality) >> (High quantity + Low quality) data**: Think about how many samples were used to 
train the model - Only 102 **high quality** human authored instances. Just with that, notice the quality of translation 
in output (D). The model has successfully learned to capture _Tagore's_ meaning in Bengali and his metaphors. This just
goes to show how important post-training is. While the model learns Bengali/English representations during pre-training,
using a well curated dataset in post helps to solve more complex tasks - translating these poems is no small feat, even 
for skilled human poets 😅.

**4B is the sweet spot**: Translations from the 0.6B variant shows why scale matters. At 600M parameters, the model is
struggling to capture meaning, let alone perform translation. This indicates that these models are really more suitable
for edge devices, rather than for attempting more complex tasks.

**With/without SFT**: Finally, we see the impact of post-training when examining the trained v.s. untrained 4B model. 
The untrained 4B (Base) Qwen _kind of_ gets the translation correct. However, it does so by interpreting the poem 
**literally**. For example, it distorts the metaphor of the _unchained mind_,
- Untrained: _boundaries of the house are not within the courtyard_. 👎
- Trained: _where the walls are down and the sky is the roof_. 👍

This just lends further credence to the positive effect of post-training.
